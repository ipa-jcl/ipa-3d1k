Abstract

The rapid evolution of retail robotics is set to transform in-store operations through advanced automation, spanning vision-based inventory tracking, order picking, packing, and restocking. Yet fine-grained product identification remains a bottleneck: assortments change, packaging evolves, and shelves host thousands of near-duplicate items, requiring perception systems that can adapt quickly with minimal setup.
This paper addresses this challenge with two key contributions. First, we present a semi-automated, robot-assisted acquisition pipeline that records 3D scene ground truth via iterative object placement and projects it into each captured image, producing dense and scalable annotations at low cost. Second, we extend the IPA-3D1K dataset with challenging real-world shelf scenes containing 130 near-duplicate SKUs. While scenes are not paired one-to-one, the same product set appears across synthetic and real images, enabling controlled object-level sim-to-real analyses under occlusion, rearrangement, and lighting variation.
Using frozen DINOv3 visual features, our baseline recognition pipeline allows the product index to be updated within minutes. We evaluate training-free and fast adaptation approaches, including kNN retrieval and a lightweight classifier head, to analyze the capabilities and limitations of this representation for fine-grained retail identification. Experiments show that on the FineGrainedOCR dataset the lightweight head improves over kNN by approximately 11 percentage points, reducing the gap to fully trained models to only 1.9–5.3 pp. On IPA-3D1K with 1,000 SKUs, synthetic-scene retrieval achieves strong performance (Top-1 ≈ 90%, Top-2 ≈ 95%), while exact disambiguation among visually similar products remains challenging.
Furthermore, we observe that confidence thresholds enable targeted triage during inference, while a neighborhood-based risk signal predicts potential confusions during training, highlighting where specialized recognition modules can provide the greatest benefit.
